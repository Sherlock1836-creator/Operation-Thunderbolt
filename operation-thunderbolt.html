TOP SECRET
JUNE 1976

Operation Thunderbolt

Entebbe, 1976

June 27, 1976. Air France Flight 139 has been hijacked by Palestinian and German terrorists. The plane, with 248 passengers and crew, has been diverted first to Libya and then to Entebbe Airport in Uganda, where President Idi Amin provides shelter to the hijackers.

The terrorists separate Israeli and Jewish passengers from others. Non-Jewish hostages are released, but 106 remain captive, along with the Air France crew who refuse to abandon them.

The hijackers demand the release of 53 militants imprisoned in Israel and other countries. The deadline: July 1st. The consequence: execution of hostages.

Israel faces an impossible choice—until, armed with overhead pictures of the airport taken by a spy, information from released hostages, and brainstorming from their best military minds, they are ready to do the unthinkable—a daring raid 2,500 miles from home.

Israel managed to get the deadline extended until July 4, 1976, which happened to also be the Bicentennial of the United States. On that day, they finally had enough information, and the raid took place.

Choose your perspective in this historic rescue mission.

The Commando

You are part of the elite Sayeret Matkal unit under Yoni Netanyahu. Your mission: storm the terminal and rescue the hostages. One mistake could cost lives.

The Hostage

You are among the 106 captives held in the old terminal. Every sound could mean rescue or execution. Can you stay calm and help others survive?

The Commander

You are Major General Dan Shomron, overseeing the operation. Every decision—timing, tactics, contingencies—rests on your shoulders. Lives depend on your judgment.

July 3, 1976 - 23:00 hrs - En route to Entebbe

The C-130 Hercules transport plane vibrates as you fly in darkness toward Uganda. Around you, your fellow commandos check their weapons in silence. Some sleep. Others stare into nothing.

Lieutenant Colonel Yoni Netanyahu briefs the assault team one final time. "The old terminal. Four hostage-takers inside, maybe more. Ugandan soldiers nearby. We land, we drive the Mercedes to the terminal posing as Idi Amin's convoy, we eliminate the terrorists, we evacuate the hostages. Eleven minutes on the ground. Questions?"

You think about the plan. The intelligence suggests there are four terrorists, but rumors say there might be more. The element of surprise is everything.

What is your priority as the plane approaches?

You pull out the terminal blueprints, studying them by red light. The main hall, the exits, the likely positions of the terrorists. You visualize the assault sequence.

Yoni notices and nods approvingly. "Good. When we enter, you take the left corridor. Speed and precision."

The plane descends. You feel the wheels touch down. The rear ramp opens. The black Mercedes and Land Rovers drive out onto the Entebbe tarmac. Ugandan sentries wave—they think you're a Ugandan convoy.

The terminal is 300 meters ahead. Two sentries stand near the entrance.

This is actually what happened. Yoni tried to shoot the sentries with a silenced gun, but missed one and only succeeded in wounding the other. A commando from the Land Rover behind him managed to eliminate both, but with an unsilenced weapon.

The raiding party was afraid that they had given away the element of surprise, but that doesn't seem to have been the case.

The Mercedes continues toward the terminal. You burst through the entrance. Inside, the terrorists are caught completely off-guard despite the gunshots outside. Your team engages with deadly precision.

"Terrorists! Drop your weapons!" The shots are clean, professional. Within seconds, all four terrorists in the main hall are down.

But then—more gunfire from outside. Ugandan soldiers responding. Yoni leads a suppression team while you shepherd hostages toward the waiting C-130s.

You hear Yoni's voice over the radio: "Get them out! Move, move!" Then silence. Someone says, "Yoni's hit."

MISSION SUCCESS

All 102 hostages rescued. Four terrorists eliminated. Three hostages killed in the crossfire. One Israeli soldier killed: Lieutenant Colonel Yonatan Netanyahu, aged 30.

The unsilenced shots at the sentries caused momentary concern, but the terrorists inside remained unaware until it was too late. The raid lasted 53 minutes and became legend—a demonstration of Israel's reach and resolve.

Your actions under pressure helped save lives. You followed your training, adapted to complications, and got the hostages out. Yoni would have been proud.

This option wasn't tried—we will never know.

You decide to trust Yoni's deputy's assessment. The Mercedes drives past the sentries, who remain at attention. Perhaps they do think this is a ceremonial arrival. Perhaps they're simply confused.

But what happens next is uncertain. Would the sentries have maintained their salute long enough for the assault to begin? Would they have raised the alarm? Would avoiding their elimination have preserved the element of surprise more completely, or would it have created a threat from behind?

In the actual operation, the sentries were eliminated. That choice was made in the heat of the moment by soldiers who had to weigh split-second decisions against unknown risks.

History only records what was done, not what might have been. The path not taken remains forever a mystery.

HISTORICAL SPECULATION

This scenario represents a choice that wasn't made during the actual Operation Thunderbolt. The commandos chose to eliminate the sentries, despite complications with silenced weapons.

Would ignoring them have worked? We can never know. What we do know is that the men who made these decisions in real-time, under enormous pressure, succeeded in rescuing 102 hostages from 2,500 miles away.

Their choices—right or wrong, clean or messy—saved lives. That's what matters.

You fieldstrip your Uzi, checking every component. Magazine loaded. Bolt action smooth. Backup magazine secured. You're ready.

The plane touches down. The operation begins. The Mercedes drives out with you inside, heading toward the terminal. Behind you, more commandos in Land Rovers.

You see the terminal ahead—a white building glowing in the darkness. Two Ugandan sentries lounge near the entrance, rifles slung.

You approach Yoni. "Sir, if there are more terrorists than we think, or if Ugandan forces respond faster—"

Yoni cuts you off, but not harshly. "We adapt. That's why you're here. Keep your eyes open, communicate what you see."

Minutes later, you're on Ugandan soil. The Mercedes carries your team toward the terminal. Everything seems quiet. Too quiet.

You spot the sentries. Then, through the terminal windows, you see shadows moving. More than four.

You radio the update to Yoni and all teams. "Possible additional hostiles inside. Adjusting for six to eight targets."

Yoni acknowledges: "Good work. Teams, weapons hot. We adapt."

The terminal is 300 meters ahead. Two sentries stand near the entrance.

The Mercedes screeches to a halt at the terminal entrance. Doors burst open. Your team pours out, weapons up.

"Terrorists! Drop your weapons!" someone shouts in Hebrew and Arabic.

Two terrorists reach for their guns. Your training takes over. Precise shots. They fall. Inside the terminal, screaming. Hostages hit the floor. Two more terrorists appear from a side room—you and your team engage.

Eleven seconds. Four terrorists neutralized. But then—gunfire from outside. Ugandan soldiers.

Yoni leads a team to suppress them. You hear his voice: "Get the hostages moving! Now!"

You shepherd terrified civilians toward the waiting C-130s. Children crying, adults stumbling. Behind you, sustained gunfire. Then—silence.

Someone says, "Yoni's hit."

MISSION SUCCESS

All 102 hostages rescued. Four terrorists eliminated. Three hostages killed in the crossfire. One Israeli soldier killed: Lieutenant Colonel Yonatan Netanyahu, aged 30.

The raid lasted 53 minutes. It became legend—a demonstration of Israel's reach and resolve. But the price was Yoni's life, and he would never see his son grow up.

Your actions under pressure helped save lives. You followed your training, adapted to threats, and got the hostages out. Yoni would have been proud.

You order the vehicles to stop 100 meters short. "We go on foot!"

The team dismounts and approaches tactically. But the sentries spot you. One raises his rifle and shouts. The element of surprise is blown.

Gunfire erupts. You eliminate the sentries, but inside the terminal, the terrorists have been alerted. They grab hostages as human shields.

A firefight breaks out. Your team is skilled, but the terrorists are desperate. You breach the terminal, but by the time you secure it, the damage is done.

PARTIAL SUCCESS

Mission compromised. 89 hostages rescued, but 13 killed during the firefight. Two Israeli soldiers wounded. The terrorists were eliminated, but the loss of hostages weighs heavily.

The early approach sacrificed the element of surprise. In special operations, seconds and deception matter. The mission succeeded, but at a higher cost than necessary.

You barrel through at full speed. The sentries barely have time to react before the Mercedes is past them. But the noise alerts everyone inside.

You crash through the terminal entrance. Terrorists spin around, weapons raised. You fire first, but one terrorist gets off a burst into the hostage area.

The terminal becomes chaos. Your team clears the terrorists, but several hostages are wounded. The Ugandan forces respond quickly—too quickly. You're pinned down.

Yoni makes the call: "Evacuate now!" Your team fights its way back to the planes with the hostages. It's messy, costly, but you get out.

COSTLY SUCCESS

All terrorists eliminated. 95 hostages rescued. Seven hostages killed, five wounded. Two Israeli soldiers killed, including Yoni Netanyahu. Four soldiers wounded.

The mission succeeded, but the lack of stealth turned the operation into a direct assault. Speed alone cannot replace tactical precision. The rescued hostages made it home, but the human cost was high.

You decide to wait, not wanting to cause confusion with an unconfirmed report. The Mercedes pulls up to the terminal.

But your instinct was right. As your team enters, six terrorists appear—not four. They're more organized than expected. One opens fire immediately.

Your team is caught in a crossfire. The terrorists have prepared for this. Two of your teammates fall. The hostages panic, running in all directions.

Yoni tries to rally the team, but a terrorist shoots him from an elevated position no one predicted. The operation collapses into chaos.

MISSION FAILURE

The raid fails catastrophically. 48 hostages rescued, 58 killed in the crossfire or executed. Five Israeli soldiers killed, including Yoni Netanyahu. Eight wounded.

Your hesitation to communicate critical intelligence cost lives. In special operations, information must flow instantly. Trust your instincts and your team. Silence in the face of uncertainty can be as deadly as the enemy.

Years later, people will ask you about Operation Thunderbolt. They'll want to know about the heroes, the sacrifices, the people who made it possible.

Is there anyone from your commando group that you want to make sure is not forgotten?

Yoni Netanyahu

Of course. Yoni gave his life for the success of the mission. Hopefully the popularity of the books about him and the fact that the mission was named for him posthumously will assure that his sacrifice is long remembered.

Lieutenant Colonel Yonatan "Yoni" Netanyahu was 30 years old when he died at Entebbe. He was the older brother of Benjamin Netanyahu, who would later become Prime Minister of Israel. Yoni was known for his leadership, courage, and unwavering commitment to his soldiers and his country.

The mission was renamed "Operation Yonatan" in his honor. His letters and writings were published and became bestsellers in Israel. He remains a symbol of Israeli military valor and sacrifice.

Sorin Hershko

A tragic fate, indeed. Hershko was about to be released from army service when he was sent on this mission. While clearing the airport, Hershko was shot in the neck by a Ugandan soldier, and was left paralyzed from the neck down.

With hard work, he learned to use a wheelchair, and received a medal for his sacrifice. He even went back to Entebbe many years later, but to this day he pays the price for the mission's success.

Surin Hershko never let his injury define him. He became an advocate for disabled veterans, rebuilt his life with remarkable courage, and when he finally returned to Entebbe decades later, he said he had no regrets. He understood the stakes, and he knew the hostages' lives were worth the risk.

His sacrifice reminds us that the cost of Operation Thunderbolt was not just measured in those who died, but also in those who lived with the consequences for the rest of their lives.

Dora Bloch

Her story is very, very sad, but she was a hostage, not a commando. Perhaps you can find out about her if you ask a hostage.

As a commando, you focused on the tactical aspects of the mission—the assault, the rescue, the extraction. But the hostages each had their own stories, their own struggles, their own fates.

Dora Bloch's story is one that still haunts those who remember Entebbe. If you want to understand the full human cost of this operation, you should experience it from a hostage's perspective.

Jean-Jacques Maimoni and Pasco Cohen

Their fate was also very tragic, but they were hostages, not commandos. Why not ask a hostage to find out about them?

As a commando, you were trained to focus on the mission—neutralize threats, secure hostages, extract safely. But each hostage had a name, a story, a life that was forever changed by those days in Entebbe.

Jean-Jacques Maimoni and Pasco Cohen were among those who paid the ultimate price. Their stories deserve to be told, but from the perspective of those who lived through the captivity alongside them.

June 30, 1976 - Old Terminal, Entebbe Airport

You sit on the floor of the old terminal, your back against a peeling wall. Around you, families huddle together. Children ask questions their parents can't answer. The air is thick with heat, fear, and exhaustion.

The four terrorists patrol constantly. Two Palestinians, two Germans. Automatic weapons always visible. They separated you from the non-Jewish passengers days ago. Those people are gone now—released. You remain.

Idi Amin visited yesterday, assuring everyone that he's "negotiating." But you saw him laughing with the hijackers.

A woman near you, Mrs. Davidson, whispers, "Do you think Israel will pay? Will they give in to demands?"

You've heard rumors. Some say a rescue is impossible. Others whisper that Israel never abandons its people.

The terrorists announced today is the final deadline. What do you do?

You spend your time helping others. You play games with frightened children. You share food. You tell an elderly man that help is coming, even though you don't know if it's true.

The Air France crew—bless them—refused to leave. Captain Michel Bacos said, "We stay with our passengers." Their courage helps everyone stay calm.

Days pass. The deadline looms. Then, late on July 3rd, you hear it: aircraft engines. Different from usual.

Before you can process it, explosions. Gunfire. Someone screams in Hebrew, "Get down! Israeli Defense Forces!"

You drop to the floor, covering your head. Around you, chaos. Bullets crack through the air. You hear terrorists shouting, then falling silent.

Hands grab you. "Move! Run to the plane!" You run. You don't look back.

RESCUED

You survive. You board the C-130 Hercules in a daze. You're flying home. Around you, other survivors weep with relief and grief.

Your calm presence helped prevent panic during the raid. When the shooting started, those you'd comforted followed instructions and survived. Three hostages died, but 102 made it out.

Years later, you'll learn that the raid's commander, Yoni Netanyahu, died protecting you. You'll never forget that sacrifice.

You watch the terrorists carefully. You notice patterns. One checks the perimeter every 45 minutes. Another guards the main entrance. At night, they seem more relaxed.

You memorize their positions, their weapon types, their blind spots. You don't know why you're doing this—what good is information when you're trapped?

Then, on the night of July 3rd, you hear aircraft. Unusual activity outside. The terrorists become agitated.

Suddenly—explosions. Gunfire. Israeli commandos burst in. You immediately understand the layout you memorized. "The fourth terrorist is behind that door!" you shout in Hebrew.

A commando nods and moves. Three seconds later, the threat is neutralized. "Good call," he says, hurrying you toward the exit.

RESCUED - INTELLIGENCE CONTRIBUTION

Your careful observation helped the rescue team. Your warning about the fourth terrorist's position prevented an ambush that could have killed soldiers and hostages.

You survive and return home. A military officer will later thank you personally for your "field intelligence." You helped save lives simply by staying alert and aware.

Sometimes, courage isn't about action—it's about keeping your head when everyone else is losing theirs.

You quietly approach a small group of younger men. "We should consider trying to escape," you whisper.

One man, a former soldier, shakes his head. "Too dangerous. They have automatic weapons. We'd be cut down."

Another agrees: "We wait. Israel is coming."

But a third man, desperate and angry, likes your idea. Over the next day, you develop a risky plan. You'll rush one terrorist during a shift change, take his weapon, create chaos.

On July 3rd, before you can act, you hear aircraft. Then—gunfire. Israeli commandos storm in.

But your co-conspirator, in his panic and confusion, thinks this is his moment. He lunges toward a falling terrorist's weapon. A commando, not knowing his intent, fires.

You survive the raid, but your would-be ally doesn't. The guilt haunts you.

RESCUED - WITH REGRET

You made it out, but your escape planning had unintended consequences. In a hostage situation, unauthorized action during a rescue can be catastrophic.

The commandos who rescued you were professionals executing a precise plan. When hostages deviate from "get down and stay down," they can be mistaken for threats.

You learned a hard lesson: sometimes, patience and trust in rescue forces saves more lives than desperate action. You were lucky. Your friend wasn't.

Years later, when you tell people about those terrifying days in Entebbe, they'll ask about the others who were there with you. The ones who didn't all make it home. The ones whose stories deserve to be remembered.

Is there anyone from your fellow hostages that you want to make sure is not forgotten?

Sorin Hershko

A tragic fate, indeed, but Hershko was a commando, not a hostage. You'd find out more about him if you asked a commando.

You remember hearing about him later—the young soldier who was shot during the raid and left paralyzed. But you never met him during your captivity. He was one of the brave men who came to save you.

His story belongs to the commandos who fought alongside him. If you want to understand his sacrifice, you should experience the operation from their perspective.

Dora Bloch

Her story is very, very sad. An elderly woman, she choked on something during a meal and was taken to a hospital. She was ready to return to the other hostages, but a kind doctor, thinking he was sparing her a grisly fate, kept her in the hospital.

She was still in the hospital when the rescue took place. Idi Amin was so furious at the humiliation he underwent that he ordered her dragged from her bed and executed.

Dora Bloch was 75 years old. She was a British-Israeli dual citizen and the mother-in-law of a prominent Israeli. The British government protested her murder, but Amin denied everything. Her body was never officially recovered, though witnesses later confirmed she had been killed and buried in a sugar plantation.

She survived the hijacking, survived the captivity, and was murdered in cold blood simply because a dictator needed to lash out at someone. Her death remains one of the most haunting aspects of Operation Thunderbolt.

Jean-Jacques Maimoni and Pasco Cohen

Their fate was also very tragic. When the Israeli commandos first burst into the airport terminal, they called out for all the hostages to stay down. Sadly, in the confusion, they stood up and were killed by friendly fire.

Ida Borochovitch, another hostage, was killed by one of the hijackers in the firefight.

Jean-Jacques Maimoni was 19 years old. Pasco Cohen was 52. Ida Borochovitch was 56. They survived days of terror, they were moments away from freedom, and they died in the chaos of their own rescue.

These deaths haunt everyone who was there. The commandos who came to save you did everything they could, but in the fog of war, in the darkness and confusion, not everyone could be saved. You remember their faces. You remember waiting with them. You remember the moment everything went wrong.

Their families received the news that rescue had succeeded—but not for everyone. These three hostages are why you can never call Operation Thunderbolt a perfect success, no matter how miraculous it was.

July 1, 1976 - IDF HQ, Tel Aviv

As Major General Dan Shomron, you stand in the war room studying reconnaissance photos of Entebbe Airport. The mission everyone said was impossible is now your responsibility.

Prime Minister Rabin gave final approval. Minister of Defense Peres is watching. The Chief of Staff expects success. And 106 Israeli citizens are counting on you to bring them home.

Colonel Netanyahu's team will assault the old terminal. Other units will secure the new terminal, destroy Ugandan MiGs, and control the airfield. Four C-130 Hercules will land in darkness without permission from Uganda.

Your intelligence officer reports: "Sir, we have reliable information on the terrorists' positions and schedules. But Ugandan military presence is uncertain. Could be two dozen soldiers, could be two hundred."

The assault is scheduled for July 3rd, 23:00 hours. You have 48 hours to finalize the plan.

What is your focus?

You gather all unit commanders. "We go through this minute by minute," you say, laying out the timeline.

23:00: First C-130 lands. 23:01: Vehicles roll out. 23:02: Terminal in sight. 23:03: Breach. 23:15: Hostages boarding. 23:30: Wheels up.

Yoni Netanyahu presents his team's readiness. "We're ready, sir. Eleven minutes from touchdown to hostages secure."

You drill the timing over and over. Every second matters. The Ugandans must be caught completely off-guard.

July 3rd arrives. The operation launches. The C-130s land silently. The assault team reaches the terminal... perfectly on schedule. The terrorists are caught mid-shift change, exactly as your intelligence predicted.

Eleven minutes later, Yoni radios: "Hostages secure. Moving to aircraft." Then—gunfire. A pause. "Yoni is down."

Your heart sinks, but there's no time. "Continue evacuation! All units, execute withdrawal!"

OPERATION SUCCESSFUL

Your meticulous timing gave the assault team every advantage. The terrorists were neutralized before they could react. 102 hostages rescued. Three hostages killed in the crossfire. Yoni Netanyahu killed by a Ugandan sniper during withdrawal.

The operation lasted 53 minutes. Your planning and coordination turned an impossible mission into a legendary success. But the price was high—Yoni's life, and the three hostages who didn't make it.

In war rooms around the world, military planners will study your operation for decades. But you'll always remember the cost.

You assign a dedicated team to plan for Ugandan military intervention. "Assume worst case: immediate response from their barracks, possible armored vehicles, heavy weapons."

Your team develops a suppression plan. If Ugandan forces respond, a specialized unit will engage and delay them while the hostages evacuate. You position additional commandos to create a defensive perimeter.

July 3rd. The operation begins. Your assault team hits the terminal perfectly. But as predicted, Ugandan soldiers respond within minutes. However, your suppression team is ready.

A firefight erupts, but it's controlled. Your forces have positioned themselves to create a corridor for hostage evacuation. The Ugandans are kept at bay.

All hostages board the C-130s. But during the withdrawal, Yoni Netanyahu is shot while leading a rear-guard action. He dies covering the evacuation.

TACTICAL EXCELLENCE

Your contingency planning saved the mission. When Ugandan forces responded, your teams were ready. All 102 hostages rescued. Three hostages killed during the initial assault. Yoni Netanyahu killed during withdrawal.

Your ability to plan for multiple scenarios turned potential disaster into success. The operation succeeded despite enemy response because you anticipated complications.

Leadership isn't about hoping for the best—it's about preparing for the worst.

You focus on the evacuation. "Getting in is dramatic," you tell your team. "But getting 102 panicked civilians onto aircraft in darkness, under potential fire, is where we could lose this mission."

You rehearse evacuation procedures. Medical teams positioned. Clear routes marked. Soldiers assigned to guide specific groups. Aircraft crews briefed on rapid boarding.

You even prepare for wounded hostages—stretchers, medical supplies, evacuation priorities.

July 3rd. The assault goes perfectly. Terrorists neutralized. But as predicted, some hostages are wounded. The terminal is chaos—crying children, disoriented adults, injured people.

Your preparation pays off. Your teams move smoothly, guiding hostages to aircraft. Medical teams handle casualties. Within 15 minutes, everyone is boarding.

But Yoni stayed behind to ensure everyone got out. A Ugandan sniper's bullet finds him in the darkness.

HUMANITARIAN SUCCESS

Your focus on evacuation saved lives. All 102 hostages rescued, including several who would have died without immediate medical attention. Three hostages killed during the assault. Yoni Netanyahu killed during final withdrawal.

Special operations aren't just about violence—they're about saving lives under impossible conditions. Your emphasis on evacuation procedures ensured no one was left behind and that wounded hostages received immediate care.

Yoni died doing what he believed in: ensuring every Israeli made it home. Your planning honored that commitment.

As the commander of this operation, you've seen extraordinary courage from many people—soldiers, hostages, even civilians who helped. History will remember this mission, but the question remains:

Who do you think was the biggest hero of this story?

Yoni Netanyahu

Of course that is the obvious answer—Yoni gave up his life and all of his unfulfilled dreams. Yet I find some comfort in knowing that this was exactly the way Yoni wanted to live his life.

In a 1963 letter, he wrote: "The trouble with the youth here is that their lives are meager in content. I ought to be ready at every moment of my life to confront myself and say—'This is what I've done.'"

Yoni lived by those words. Every moment of his life had meaning. Every decision reflected his values. When he led his team into Entebbe, he wasn't just following orders—he was living out his deepest convictions about duty, courage, and protecting his people.

He died at age 30, but he lived more fully in those 30 years than most people do in 80. That's not a consolation for his loss, but it's a testament to who he was. The mission bears his name now: Operation Yonatan. That's fitting. He embodied everything it represented.

Sorin Hershko

The way Sorin was shot and paralyzed by a Ugandan soldier meant that he was a hero every single day for the rest of his life, willing himself to go on, and achieving so much even in his incapacitated state.

We gave him a medal and tried to give him all the veteran benefits he needed, but nothing can compensate him for what he lost.

Yoni's heroism was concentrated in one moment—a bullet, a death, an end. Sorin's heroism has no end. Every morning he wakes up paralyzed. Every day he faces the reality of what he sacrificed. And every day he chooses to keep going.

He learned to use a wheelchair. He rebuilt his life. He advocated for other disabled veterans. Decades later, he even returned to Entebbe—not to relive trauma, but to show that the operation hadn't broken him.

When people ask me about heroes, I think of Sorin. Because heroism isn't just about what you're willing to die for—it's about what you're willing to live for, even when living is the harder choice.

Michel Bacos

The pilot of the Air France plane refused to leave the hostages, even when the hijackers allowed him to. His example gave such inspiration to all of our hostages and showed them that they have many friends in the nations of the world.

Captain Michel Bacos was French. He had no obligation to stay. The hijackers weren't targeting him—they were targeting Israelis and Jews. He could have walked away with the other non-Jewish passengers. No one would have blamed him.

But he looked at his passengers—terrified, separated from their families, facing execution—and he said no. "I will not leave my passengers," he told the hijackers. His entire crew stayed with him.

That decision changed everything for the hostages. They weren't alone. They had people who chose to stand with them, even at great personal risk. In those dark days in the terminal, Bacos and his crew provided food, comfort, and most importantly, solidarity.

When the rescue succeeded, Bacos didn't seek glory. He simply said he'd done what any decent person would do. But we both know that's not true. Most people wouldn't have done what he did. That's what made him a hero.

Ninnette Morenu

Believe it or not, we might owe her more than anyone else! Because of her non-Jewish sounding name, the hijackers allowed her to leave with the non-Jewish passengers. She had carefully observed the layout of the terminal, the positioning of the guards, and many crucial details.

She was able to sketch all that for the commandos, and her information made the military option a realistic choice.

When Ninnette was released, she could have simply gone home, grateful to have survived. Instead, she immediately went to Israeli intelligence and provided detailed drawings of the terminal—the positions of doors, windows, where the terrorists stood guard, where the hostages slept.

That intelligence was invaluable. We'd been working with architectural plans and satellite photos, but Ninnette gave us ground truth. She told us what the terrorists' routines were, when they were most alert, when they relaxed. Her information helped us refine our assault plan and probably saved lives.

Years later, her son Emanuel became one of the most decorated and capable Israeli soldiers ever. Sadly and heroically, he gave his life in battle for his people. Some of his missions are still too classified to reveal to the public.

A mother who helped save hostages, and a son who followed in her footsteps of service and sacrifice. The Morenu family embodies the best of what it means to serve something larger than yourself.
